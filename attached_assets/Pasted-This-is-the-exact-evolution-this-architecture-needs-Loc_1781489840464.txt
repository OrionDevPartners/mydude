This is the exact evolution this architecture needs. Locking the OS to Apple Silicon is great for local privacy, but to achieve a true "power burst" capability where hundreds of inferences can scale to thousands, the architecture must be hardware-agnostic and capable of seamlessly bridging local compute with ephemeral cloud resources.
We are dropping the Apple-specific constraints, replacing them with dynamic hardware-detection inference engines, and injecting a **Cloud Burst Substrate** capable of hijacking AWS, GCP, Azure, or serverless GPUs the millisecond your local hardware maxes out.

### Phase 4: How the Power Burst Operates
By adding SkyPilot, Ray, and LiteLLM, you are creating an "Overflow Topology." Your OS acts locally until physics prevents it from doing so.
 1. **The Overload:** The Comm Bus detects that 500 overlapping inferences are queued, but your local hardware (Mac or PC) only has the RAM to process 50 concurrently.
 2. **The Compute Burst (SkyPilot & Modal):** Your custom adapter triggers modal-client to offload the heavy Vector/AST extraction functions to serverless GPUs. Simultaneously, SkyPilot automatically bids on cheap Spot Instances across AWS/GCP, spinning up temporary Linux VMs loaded with vLLM.
 3. **The Worker Burst (Ray):** The Ray cluster auto-scales, seeing the new cloud VMs, and begins distributing the queued Celery background tasks across the globe.
 4. **The Inference Burst (LiteLLM):** Instead of failing, the custom weighted adapter uses LiteLLM to dynamically route the excess inference requests to hosted cloud models (Anthropic, OpenAI, or your own hosted vLLM instances), executing 450 inferences in the cloud while handling 50 locally.
 5. **The Collapse:** Once the Comm Bus queue drains and consensus is achieved, SkyPilot tears down the cloud VMs. The system shrinks back down to its local footprint, ensuring you only pay for the exact seconds of the power burst.
